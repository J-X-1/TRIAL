Texas law regulates replacement of existing life insurance policies through Chapter 1114 by setting minimum conduct standards for insurers and agents, requiring disclosures and notices, imposing supervision and recordkeeping duties, and providing penalties for noncompliance.

An “accelerated benefit” is “a benefit paid to an insured instead of a portion of a death benefit.” Sec. 1111.051(1).

An insurer may pay an accelerated benefit under an individual or group term life insurance policy or certificate if:

1. the insurer has received a written medical opinion, satisfactory to the insurer, that the insured has:
   - a terminal illness, Sec. 1111.052(1)(A);
   - a long-term care illness, Sec. 1111.052(1)(B); or
   - an illness or physical condition likely to cause permanent disability or premature death, including:
     - AIDS, Sec. 1111.052(1)(C)(i);
     - a malignant tumor, Sec. 1111.052(1)(C)(ii);
     - a condition requiring an organ transplant, Sec. 1111.052(1)(C)(iii); or
     - coronary artery disease resulting in acute infarction or requiring surgery, Sec. 1111.052(1)(C)(iv); and

2. the amount of the accelerated benefit is deducted from:
   - the death benefit payable under the policy or certificate, Sec. 1111.052(2)(A); and
   - any amount the insured would otherwise be entitled to convert to an individual contract, Sec. 1111.052(2)(B).

Relevant definitions:
- “Terminal illness” means an illness or physical condition, including a physical injury, that can reasonably be expected to result in death within not more than two years. Sec. 1111.051(4).
- “Long-term care illness” means an illness or physical condition that results in the inability to perform the activities of daily life or the substantial and material duties of any occupation. Sec. 1111.051(3).
- “Death benefit” means a benefit payable to a beneficiary on the death of an insured. Sec. 1111.051(2).
